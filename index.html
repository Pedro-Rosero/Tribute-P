A Tribute to Fyodor Dostoevsky

One of the greatest novelist of all time

Numerous literary critics regard him as one of the greatest novelists in all of world literature, as many of his works are considered highly influential masterpieces. Dostoevsky's literary works explore the human condition in the troubled political, social, and spiritual atmospheres of 19th-century Russia, and engage with a variety of philosophical and religious themes. His most acclaimed novels include Crime and Punishment (1866), The Idiot (1869), Demons (1872), The Adolescent (1875), and The Brothers Karamazov (1880). His 1864 novella Notes from Underground is considered to be one of the first works of existentialist literature

A timeline of his life:

    Early Life and Education
  • 1821: Born on November 11 (October 30, Old Style) in Moscow, Russia, to Mikhail and Maria Dostoevsky. His father was a doctor, and his family lived near the Mariinsky Hospital for the Poor.
  • 1837: His mother died of tuberculosis. Shortly afterward, Dostoevsky and his brother were sent to St. Petersburg to attend the Military Engineering Academy.
  • 1839: His father died, reportedly murdered by his own serfs, though the exact circumstances remain unclear.
    Early Literary Career
  • 1844: Left the military academy to pursue writing full-time.
  • 1846: Published his first novel, "Poor Folk", a socially conscious work that brought him initial literary success.
  • 1849: Arrested for participating in the Petrashevsky Circle, a group of intellectuals discussing banned political literature. He was sentenced to death, but the sentence was commuted to hard labor at the last minute.
    Years in Siberia
  • 1849–1854: Served four years in a Siberian prison camp in Omsk, followed by compulsory military service in Semipalatinsk.
  • 1854: Released from prison but remained in exile as a soldier. This period deeply influenced his views on human suffering, faith, and redemption.
  • 1857: Married Maria Isayeva, his first wife.
    Return to Writing
  • 1859: Returned to St. Petersburg and resumed his literary career.
  • 1861: Published "House of the Dead", a semi-autobiographical account of his time in prison, which gained critical acclaim.
  • 1864: Published "Notes from Underground", a philosophical novella considered a precursor to existentialist literature.
  • 1864: Endured personal tragedy with the deaths of both his wife Maria and his brother Mikhail
    Peak Literary Achievements
  • 1866: Released his masterpiece "Crime and Punishment", exploring themes of morality, guilt, and redemption.
  • 1867: Married his second wife, Anna Snitkina, a stenographer who became a devoted partner and collaborator.
  • 1868–1869: Published "The Idiot", one of his most profound works about the concept of a "truly good man."
  • 1872: Published "Demons" (also known as The Possessed), a political novel examining radical ideologies.
    Later Years and Masterpiece
  • 1879–1880: Published his final and greatest novel, "The Brothers Karamazov", a monumental work exploring faith, free will, and morality.
  • 1880: Delivered a famous speech at the unveiling of Alexander Pushkin's statue in Moscow, solidifying his status as one of Russia's greatest writers.
    Death and Legacy
  • 1881: Died on February 9 (January 28, Old Style) in St. Petersburg from complications of emphysema and epilepsy. He was 59 years old.
  • 1881: Buried in the Tikhvin Cemetery at the Alexander Nevsky Monastery, St. Petersburg.
